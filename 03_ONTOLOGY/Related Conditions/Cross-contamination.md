
# Cross-contamination

Cross-contamination refers to the unintended transfer of [[Gluten]] from gluten-containing foods or environments to foods that are intended to be gluten-free.

For individuals with [[Gluten-related disorders]], even very small amounts of gluten introduced through cross-contamination can trigger immune responses or symptoms.

## Common sources of cross-contamination

Cross-contamination can occur at multiple stages of the food chain:

### Agricultural production
- Shared harvesting equipment
- Mixed grain storage
- Transport of multiple cereals

These factors often lead to contamination of crops such as [[Oats]] with [[Wheat]], [[Barley]], or [[Rye]].

### Food processing
- Shared milling facilities
- Shared food production lines
- Packaging facilities processing multiple grains

### Food preparation environments
- Shared toasters
- Cutting boards
- Frying oil used for breaded foods
- Kitchen utensils and surfaces

These risks are particularly relevant in restaurants and shared households.

## Cross-contamination in oats

[[Oats]] are one of the most common examples of cross-contamination with [[Gluten]].

Because oats are frequently grown, transported, and processed alongside [[Wheat]], [[Barley]], and [[Rye]], most conventional oats contain measurable gluten contamination.

For this reason, individuals with [[Coeliac disease]] are typically advised to consume only **certified gluten-free oats**, which are produced using dedicated gluten-free agricultural and processing systems.

## Biological relevance

Even small quantities of [[Gluten]] introduced through cross-contamination may lead to:

- [[Immune activation]]
- [[T-cell activation]]
- [[Intestinal Inflammation]]

In people with [[Coeliac disease]], repeated exposure to contaminated foods can contribute to:

- [[Villous atrophy]]
- [[Malabsorption]]
- persistence of symptoms despite a gluten-free diet
