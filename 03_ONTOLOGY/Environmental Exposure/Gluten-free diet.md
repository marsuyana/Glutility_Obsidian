
# Gluten-free diet

## Definition

A gluten-free diet is a dietary pattern that excludes [[Gluten]] and all foods derived from gluten-containing grains.

It is the primary treatment for [[Coeliac disease]] and is also used in the management of other [[Gluten-related disorders]].

The diet requires strict avoidance of gluten in foods, beverages, and contaminated products.

---

## Gluten-containing grains to avoid

The following grains contain gluten and must be avoided:

- [[Wheat]]
- [[Barley]]
- [[Rye]]
- [[Triticale]]

Foods derived from these grains typically contain [[Gluten]].

---

## Naturally gluten-free foods

Many foods do not naturally contain gluten and are generally safe when not contaminated.

Examples include:

- Rice
- Corn
- Potatoes
- Legumes
- Fruits
- Vegetables
- Meat
- Fish
- Eggs
- Dairy products

However, contamination during processing or preparation may introduce [[Gluten]].

---

## Oats

[[Oats]] do not naturally contain gluten but may be contaminated with gluten-containing grains during harvesting, processing, or transport.

Only certified gluten-free oats are generally considered safe for individuals with [[Coeliac disease]].

Some individuals may also react to the oat protein [[Avenin]].

---

## Cross-contamination prevention

Individuals following a gluten-free diet must take precautions to avoid [[Cross-contamination]].

Common prevention strategies include:

- Separate cooking utensils
- Dedicated cooking surfaces
- Separate toasters
- Careful food labeling checks
- Avoiding shared preparation areas

---

## Medical importance

Strict adherence to a gluten-free diet allows intestinal healing in individuals with [[Coeliac disease]].

Benefits may include:

- Reduction of [[Intestinal Inflammation]]
- Recovery from [[Villous atrophy]]
- Improved nutrient absorption
- Prevention of long-term complications

---

## Challenges

Maintaining a gluten-free diet can be difficult due to:

- Hidden gluten in processed foods
- Cross-contamination risks
- Limited food availability
- Social and economic barriers

Education and support are often necessary for successful long-term adherence.
