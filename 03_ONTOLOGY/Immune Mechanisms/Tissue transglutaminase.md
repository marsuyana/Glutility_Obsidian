
Tissue transglutaminase (tTG) is an enzyme found in many tissues of the body, including the lining of the [[Small intestine]].

It plays a normal role in **protein modification, tissue repair, and wound healing**.

## Interaction with gluten

When [[Gluten]] is digested, fragments of [[Gliadins]] remain in the intestine.

The enzyme **tissue transglutaminase** modifies certain gliadin peptides through a process called **deamidation**.

This modification increases their ability to bind to genetic immune receptors such as:

- [[HLA-DQ2]]
    
- [[HLA-DQ8]]
    

## Role in coeliac disease

In genetically susceptible individuals, the immune system begins to recognize these modified peptides as foreign.

This triggers activation of the adaptive immune system, including:

- [[T-cell activation]]
    
- inflammation of the intestinal lining
    

During this process, the immune system may mistakenly produce antibodies against tissue transglutaminase itself, known as [[tTG antibodies]].

These antibodies are known as:

- [[tTG antibodies]]
    

## Mechanistic pathway

[[Gluten]]  
→ [[Gliadins]]  
→ [[33-mer gliadin peptide]]  
→ modification by [[Tissue transglutaminase]]  
→ binding to [[HLA-DQ2]] / [[HLA-DQ8]]  
→ [[T-cell activation]]  
→ intestinal inflammation  
→ [[Villous atrophy]]  
→ [[Malabsorption]]
