
Antigen presentation is the process by which immune cells display peptide fragments on their surface for recognition by T cells.

In [[Coeliac disease]], antigen presentation plays a central role in triggering the adaptive immune response to gluten-derived peptides.

---

## Presentation of gluten peptides

During digestion of [[Gluten]], fragments derived from [[Gliadin]] are produced.

Some of these peptides — particularly the [[33-mer gliadin peptide]] — resist degradation and persist in the intestinal lumen.

These peptides can cross the intestinal barrier when [[Intestinal permeability]] increases.

---

## Modification by tissue transglutaminase

Before immune recognition occurs, gluten peptides may be modified by the enzyme [[Tissue transglutaminase]].

This modification, called deamidation, increases the peptide's ability to bind to specific immune receptors.

---

## Role of HLA molecules

The modified peptides are presented to immune cells by genetic molecules known as:

- [[HLA-DQ2]]
- [[HLA-DQ8]]

These molecules are part of the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) and are strongly associated with susceptibility to [[Coeliac disease]].

---

## Activation of T cells

When gluten peptides are presented by HLA molecules, gluten-specific T cells become activated.

This leads to:

- [[T-cell activation]]
- release of inflammatory cytokines
- recruitment of immune cells

---

## Consequences in coeliac disease

The immune response triggered by antigen presentation contributes to intestinal damage seen in [[Coeliac disease]].

This includes:

- [[Intestinal Inflammation]]
- [[Villous atrophy]]
- [[Malabsorption]]
