
Tight junctions are specialized protein complexes that connect adjacent epithelial cells.

They form a critical component of the intestinal barrier and regulate the movement of substances between cells in the intestinal lining.

In the small intestine, tight junctions help control what passes from the gut lumen into the underlying tissues and bloodstream.

---

## Role in the intestinal barrier

The intestinal barrier consists of epithelial cells that are sealed together by tight junctions.

Under normal conditions, tight junctions tightly regulate what molecules can move between intestinal epithelial cells.

This prevents potentially harmful substances such as microbial products or dietary antigens from entering the body.

This protective function is part of the normal regulation of [[Intestinal permeability]].

---

## Regulation by zonulin

The protein [[Zonulin]] can regulate tight junction opening.

When zonulin signaling increases, tight junctions may temporarily loosen.

This process can increase [[Intestinal permeability]].

Exposure to gluten-derived peptides such as the [[33-mer gliadin peptide]] may stimulate zonulin release in susceptible individuals.

---

## Consequences of tight junction disruption

When tight junction integrity is disrupted, larger molecules can pass through the intestinal barrier.

This may allow gluten peptides derived from [[Gliadin]] to reach immune cells within the intestinal mucosa.

This process may contribute to immune activation in [[Coeliac disease]].
