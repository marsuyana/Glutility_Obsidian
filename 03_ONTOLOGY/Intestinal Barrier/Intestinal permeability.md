
**Intestinal permeability** refers to the ability of substances to pass through the intestinal epithelial barrier.

Under normal conditions, tightly regulated [[Tight junctions]] between intestinal epithelial cells control what can move from the intestinal lumen into the bloodstream.

## Biological regulation

One of the key regulators of intestinal permeability is [[Zonulin]].

Exposure to gluten-derived peptides such as the [[33-mer gliadin peptide]] can stimulate zonulin release, which temporarily loosens tight junctions between intestinal cells.

This allows larger molecules to pass between cells.

## Role in coeliac disease

In individuals with genetic susceptibility, increased intestinal permeability allows gluten peptides to cross the intestinal barrier and interact with immune cells.

This process contributes to the activation of immune pathways involved in [[Coeliac disease]].

## Mechanistic pathway

[[Gliadins]]  
→ [[33-mer gliadin peptide]]  
→ [[Zonulin]] release  
→ [[Intestinal permeability]]  
→ presentation by [[HLA-DQ2]]  
→ [[T-cell activation]]  
→ autoimmune damage in [[Coeliac disease]]
