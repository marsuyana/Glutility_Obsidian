
The gut microbiome refers to the community of microorganisms that live in the gastrointestinal tract.

These microorganisms interact with the intestinal barrier, immune system, and metabolism.

---

## Role in immune regulation

The gut microbiome plays an important role in regulating immune responses within the intestine.

Changes in microbial composition may influence:

- [[Intestinal permeability]]
- [[Immune activation]]
- [[Inflammation]]

---

## Interaction with the intestinal barrier

Microbial signals can affect the integrity of the intestinal barrier.

This may involve mechanisms related to:

- [[Zonulin]]
- [[Tight junctions]]
- [[Intestinal permeability]]

---

## Microbial metabolism of gluten

Some intestinal microbes are capable of degrading components of [[Gluten]].

These microbial enzymes may partially break down resistant peptides such as the [[33-mer gliadin peptide]].

Microbial processing of gluten may influence:

- the persistence of immunogenic gluten fragments
- the level of exposure of the immune system to [[Gliadin]] peptides
- the intensity of [[Immune activation]]

Differences in microbial composition may therefore affect susceptibility to [[Coeliac disease]].
## Possible role in gluten-related disorders

Alterations in the gut microbiome have been observed in individuals with [[Coeliac disease]].

These changes may influence immune responses to [[Gluten]] and [[Gliadin]].
