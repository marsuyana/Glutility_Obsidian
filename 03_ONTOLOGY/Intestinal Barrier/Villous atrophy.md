
**Villous atrophy** refers to the flattening or loss of the normal finger-like projections (villi) that line the small intestine.

These structures are essential for nutrient absorption because they greatly increase the surface area of the intestinal lining.

## Role in coeliac disease

In [[Coeliac disease]], immune activation triggered by [[Gluten]] leads to inflammation of the intestinal lining.

This inflammatory response damages the [[Intestinal villi]], causing them to become shortened or flattened.

As the villi deteriorate, the intestine becomes less efficient at absorbing nutrients.

## Consequences

Villous atrophy can lead to:

- [[Malabsorption]]
- nutrient deficiencies
- weight loss
- gastrointestinal symptoms

## Diagnostic significance

Villous atrophy is one of the key findings observed during **intestinal biopsy**, which is often used to confirm the diagnosis of [[Coeliac disease]].

The severity of villous damage is typically classified using the [[Marsh classification]] system.
## Mechanistic pathway

[[T-cell activation]]  
→ [[Intestinal Inflammation]]  
→ damage to [[Intestinal villi]]  
→ [[Villous atrophy]]  
→ [[Malabsorption]]  
→ symptoms of [[Coeliac disease]]
