## Definition

The 33-mer gliadin peptide is a highly immunogenic fragment derived from the digestion of [[Gliadin]], a component of [[Gluten]].

It is considered one of the most important peptides involved in triggering immune responses in [[Coeliac disease]].

---

## Origin

During digestion of gluten proteins, gliadin molecules are broken into peptide fragments.

One fragment, consisting of 33 amino acids, is particularly resistant to gastrointestinal enzymes.

Because it is not easily degraded, it can persist in the small intestine and interact with the immune system.

---

## Modification by tissue transglutaminase

The enzyme [[Tissue transglutaminase]] modifies the 33-mer gliadin peptide through deamidation.

This process increases the peptide's ability to bind to genetic immune receptors such as:

- [[HLA-DQ2]]
- [[HLA-DQ8]]

---

## Immune activation

When presented by HLA molecules, the 33-mer peptide activates gluten-specific T cells.

This leads to:

- [[T-cell activation]]
- release of inflammatory cytokines
- [[Intestinal Inflammation]]

---

## Role in coeliac disease

The immune response to the 33-mer peptide contributes to intestinal damage seen in [[Coeliac disease]], including:

- [[Villous atrophy]]
- [[Malabsorption]]
